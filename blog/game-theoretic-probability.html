Game-Theoretic Probability and the Emergence of Probability

Rajeev Verma

Work in progress

I've spent nearly the whole of my PhD understanding "probability," and these have been some of the most intellectually rich years I've lived. But despite my best attempts to not engage with the philosophical question of what is probability, my body goes into an anxiety mode as soon as I read or hear someone use the term "probability", "uncertainty", "random", etc. So I have to do the work of culling this anxiety, and I believe the game-theoretic way of understanding probability helps me a bit. In this note, I'll try to reason why that is.

It is argued that if one wants to do probability rigorously, it has to be done measure-theoretically. And granted, the measure-theoretic way of doing probability is something that one needs to experience at least once. But the reason it became the standard framework is that it abstracted away the word "probability" into measure, without really pinning out what it is and what meaning it carries in the real world. One does not deal with the philosophical question of what probability is when working measure-theoretically. It is an axiomatic framework that gives rigorous calculus for probabilistic reasoning — once one has already committed to a meaning. But probabilistic reasoning is a commonplace affair, partly due to the under-determinism imposed by quantum mechanics onto Newtonian determinism. Some things cannot be known exactly, and so the words go together: things are uncertain, things are random, and probability theory allows one to reason with under-determinism rigorously. The probability of raining tomorrow is \(70\%\), we say.

Random, uncertain, probable. My confusion and anxiety emerges from the blatant conflation of everything happening in that sentence. What I've really started to like and appreciate about mathematics now is how much care it takes to find good and unambiguous definitions for basic things. But measure-theoretic probability theory, as far as I can get it, never defines these terminologies — yet they go together colloquially, and that is the source of the confusion.

A Brief Overview of Measure-Theoretic Probability

Measure theory starts with what is referred to as a measure space \((\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})\). \(\Omega\) is the sample space: for a coin flip it is \(\{H, T\}\), and for two consecutive flips it is \(\{HH, HT, TH, TT\}\). \(\mathcal{F}\) is the \(\sigma\)-algebra — it consists of all the possible inquiries one can make of the experiment, for instance "at least one head" \(\{HH, HT, TH\}\) or "no tails" \(\{HH\}\). And \(\mathbb{P}\) is the probability measure, which assigns a number to each event: \(\mathbb{P}\left(\{HH, HT, TH\}\right) = \alpha\). So there is \(\alpha \in [0,1]\) probability mass assigned to the event "at least one heads." But how should one read this statement? Is the occurrence of at least one heads random? Is it uncertain, with \(\alpha\) quantifying that randomness or uncertainty?

Randomness as a word evokes the feeling that it is a property of the experiment — by extrapolation, a property of the world. Uncertainty implies it is a property of the particular observer and their experimental design. Randomness lives in the world; uncertainty manifests in who is observing the world. Neither probability theory nor its measure-theoretic presentation ever makes this distinction. But we need to, or at least to be aware that a distinction exists — that is what mathematics is for. I reckon the framework of game-theoretic probability as pioneered by Glenn Shafer and Vladimir Vovk, building on the work of Bruno De Finetti and Jean Ville, provides the necessary resolution — or at least side-steps the whole distinction more gracefully than measure-theory does. The key fact I'll base the rest of this note on is that in the game-theoretic framework, probability does not need to be defined — it emerges from the setup. And this statement does most of the heavy-lifting to cull my anxiety.

The Setup of Game-Theoretic Probability

There is an experimenter trying to understand the world. The experimenter could be a scientist aiming to understand coastal tides — an independent phenomenon manifesting in the world entirely beyond their control. They can collect data, theorise, and come up with some understanding of its occurrence. Such a theory is said to be good if its predictions are consistent with the tides' actual behaviour. The game-theoretic framework is rooted exactly in this idea — observing a phenomenon, collecting data to explain it, and producing explanations that stay true to the phenomenon. Except it uses the word Sceptic for the experimenter, and World for the phenomenon of interest.

For simplicity, consider data for tide (1) or no tide (0) on consecutive days: \(y_1, y_2, y_3, \ldots\) with \(y_t \in \{0,1\}\). Let \(\Omega\) be all possible such sequences — the same sample space as before. Define a function \(x: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}\), called the variable, which plays the role of the random variable. It represents one possible query the Sceptic wishes to ask about the world. For instance, \(x = \mathbf{1}_{E}\) where \(E = \{0,1,0,1,\ldots;\; 1,0,1,0,\ldots\}\) asks whether the tide alternates every day. The question then becomes: when can the Sceptic be said to truly understand the World?

The Betting Formulation

Here comes what I would call the elegant move of this framework, borrowed from early developments in probability in betting games, from De Finetti, and from finance. Imagine the Sceptic selling the variable \(x\) to some opponent — what price can they charge? Posed differently: imagine owning a black-box with a reward inside that you cannot see, but about which you have a hypothesis. An opponent wants to buy the box, and you must commit to paying them whatever is inside. What price would you charge so that the transaction doesn't put you at a loss? The variable \(x\) is precisely that black-box — its value is dictated by the World \(\xi \in \Omega\), out of the Sceptic's control.

The answer is obvious at one extreme. With no understanding whatsoever, the safe price is \(\sup_{\xi \in \Omega} x\!\left(\xi\right)\). But the Sceptic is not merely selling the black-box — they are also staking a claim to understanding the World. This is the second elegant move. The Sceptic embodies their understanding through a betting strategy \(\mathcal{P}\), which formalises the claim that they can predict what the World will do next. This leads to the following protocol:

  • Sceptic starts with initial wealth \(K_0\) and strategy \(\mathcal{P}\).
  • for \(\;t = 1, 2, 3, \ldots\)
  • Sceptic places stake \(m_t\) according to \(\mathcal{P}\)
  • World reveals \(y_t\)
  • Capital updates: \(\;K^{\mathcal{P}}_t = K^{\mathcal{P}}_{t-1} + m_t \cdot y_t\)

If the strategy aligns with the World's moves, the Sceptic accumulates wealth. Once the World has revealed the complete path \(\xi\), the final capital is \(K^{\mathcal{P}}(\xi)\). For \(\mathcal{P}\) to be a good strategy, the Sceptic needs to ensure:

\[ K^{\mathcal{P}}(\xi) \;\geq\; x\!\left(\xi\right) - \alpha, \qquad \forall\, \xi \in \Omega. \]

Let's unpack this. When the Sceptic commits to sell \(x\) for price \(\alpha\), they commit to paying \(x(\xi)\) for every possible realisation \(\xi\). The expression above says that, using strategy \(\mathcal{P}\), the Sceptic can generate enough capital that the commitment goes through for any \(\xi\). Hence \(\alpha\) is the price they need to charge. The better their understanding of the World — the better the strategy — the lower the price they can offer, since their capital does more of the covering. It is from this competitive pricing, from this narrowing of what the World might do, that probability emerges.
